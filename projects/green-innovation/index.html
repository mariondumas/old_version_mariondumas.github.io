Directing technological change for sustainable growth:
Unlocking green innovation

We call "locked" technologies and systems of production that are in a stable configuration because many elements complement and reinforce each other, making it hard to dislodge and reform. For example, the combusion engine is locked in most obviously by the extensive infrastructure of fueling stations, but also a myriad of economic investments and behaviors that were fine-tuned to complement the combusion engine. When attempting to direct technological change to meet ecological constraints, radical change is needed. Hence we must understand how economic systems get unlocked. What does it take so that the network of interdependencies between technological components, firms, institutions and consumer behaviors loosens and allows change? How can environmental and industrial policies that attempt to trigger these changes take into account these interdependencies and the difficult task of coordinating a network of actors who must complementary investments to bring about the desired change?
